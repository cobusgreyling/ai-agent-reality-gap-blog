A new study puts a frame around something I have written about before…the ***growing distance between*** how individuals use AI and how their institutions respond to it.

The paper is aimed at education but the argument is not confined to it.

## The Numbers that tell the story

ChatGPT now sees roughly 800 million weekly users and about 2.63 billion messages per day.

About 10% of those messages relate to tutoring and teaching.

That makes a consumer chatbot one of the largest pieces of de facto education infrastructure in the world.

Meanwhile…

US teachers using AI for work jumped from 18% in fall 2023 to 60% in 2024–25.

And, as of September 2025, only 19% of higher-education institutions had a formal AI policy in place.

Over 80% of students say their teachers have never explicitly taught them how to use AI.

The users are decades ahead of the institutions. That is the reality gap.

## Power to the people: How LLMs flip the script on technology diffusion

Andrej Karpahti wrote about this an a blog post…how transformative technologies usually follow a top-down diffusion path…

Originating in government or military contexts, passing through corporations, and eventually reaching individuals.

Think electricity, cryptography, computers, flight, the internet, or GPS.

This progression feels intuitive, new and powerful technologies are usually scarce, capital-intensive, and their use requires specialised technical expertise in the early stages.

But, AI (LLMs) flipped the script on technology diffusion…

## The reason agents widen the gap

Generative AI produced text.

The institution could ignore it, ban it, or wrap a policy around the output.

Agentic systems act.

They plan steps, call tools, transact and complete multi-step goals on a user's behalf.

The blast radius of an action is larger than the blast radius of a generation.

Governance built for the latter cannot supervise the former.

I guesst he same dynamic applies to the enterprise.

Employees are already running agents in the open, sometimes through sanctioned copilots, often through consumer tools that touch nothing the security team can see.

And, seemingly the institutional response is still being drafted.

## Gaps everywhere

Because of the bottom up diffusion of AI, gaps are popping up all over…governance and management is not keeping up with academic and enterprise worker use.

Also in enterprise, the gap is there defined by MIT as the shadow AI economy. With management supplying AI tools employees do not want to use. And opting to bring their own.

There is a safety gap, with personal agents presenting an extremely dynamic attach surface for nefarious attacks. This is largely to the naivety of AI Agents

There is gap in how AI is being marketed and the hype, with a serous disconnect to how it is implemented in reality.

And the list goes on…

## The three-tension framework

Any serious agentic deployment has to balance three tensions that pull against each other.

### Implementation Feasibility

Can this actually run, safely and sustainably, inside the real workflows of the people who will use it?

Not "does it demo well."

Not "does the model pass the eval."

Can it be operated, monitored, paid for, and supported at scale.

In agent terms: tool-call safety, fallback behaviour, observability, cost per task, the support burden on whoever owns it.

### Adaptation Speed

Can the institution learn fast enough to keep pace with the model layer below it?

Model capability moves on a monthly cadence.

Policy, training, assessment design & procurement move on an annual one, sometimes slower.

The gap between those two clocks is where shadow usage grows.

### Mission Alignment

Does the deployment uphold the values the organisation says it has…equity, transparency, pedagogical integrity, or whatever the enterprise equivalent is?

This is the tension that gets paved over when speed wins.

It is also the one that survives the longest after a project ships.

## Tensions interact

This is the part I t think most rollout frameworks miss.

Push speed without feasibility and trust collapses.

Push feasibility & alignment without speed and usage moves underground, where you can no longer see it at all.

You cannot maximise all three. You can only choose where to sit.

The framework's value is that it forces the tradeoff to be made on purpose rather than by accident.

## Deployments

The line below is from the paper…

> Build monitoring and evaluation in from the beginning so deployments function as tested hypotheses, not one-off installations.

Every agent rollout is a claim about the world, that this tool, in these hands, will produce this outcome.

Treating it as an installation hides the claim.

Treating it as a hypothesis exposes it to evidence.

This is the same argument I have made about evals, telemetry and human-in-the-loop on consequential actions.

## Not acting

If teachers and education leaders do not decide where agents belong, others will decide for them because vendors are optimising for engagement, regulators reacting to the last headline, students and staff routing around governance entirely.

One canargue the same is true for every vertical.

If the institution does not choose, the vendor, the regulator, or the shadow user fills the vacuum.

None of those three optimises for the institution's mission.

## Thinking of the future…

Agentic AI is moving from scattered pilots to quiet backbone, on the same arc that took online shopping, the smartphone, and the internet itself from dismissed to load-bearing.

The choice now is not whether agents arrive inside institutions. They are already there.

The choice is whether they arrive on the institution's terms or on someone else's.

The three-tension framework is one of the cleaner instruments for making that choice deliberately.
